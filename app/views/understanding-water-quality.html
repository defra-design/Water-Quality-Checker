Understanding water quality

Plain English explanations of the factors that affect whether you can feel confident using open water.

  1. Six themes
  2. Water quality indicators

Six themes

Water Intelligence brings information together across these themes. Each affects the answer to: would I feel confident using the water here today?

Weather

Weather affects water conditions quickly and noticeably.

  • Rainfall – washes pollutants from roads, fields and urban areas into rivers. Heavy rain can also trigger storm overflows.
  • Temperature – warmer water encourages algae growth and affects comfort for swimmers.
  • Storms – combine heavy rain, high flow and overflow risk.
  • Drought – low flow can concentrate pollutants and reduce oxygen levels.
Hydrology

Hydrology is the movement and levels of water in rivers, lakes and underground.

  • River level – how high the water is. Unusually high levels can be dangerous near the bank.
  • River flow – how fast water is moving. High flow after rain is normal.
  • Groundwater – water beneath the surface that feeds rivers during dry periods.
  • Reservoir levels – important for water supply and some recreational access.
Pollution

Pollution enters water from many sources. Not all pollution is visible.

  • Storm overflows – relief valves at sewage works that operate during heavy rain.
  • Industrial incidents – spills and discharges from factories and treatment plants.
  • Agricultural pollution – runoff containing fertilisers, soil and animal waste.
  • Recent pollution reports – incidents reported by the public or detected by monitoring.
Water chemistry

Chemical measurements help scientists assess water health and bathing safety.

  • Oxygen – essential for fish and aquatic life.
  • Nutrients – nitrates and phosphates from farming can fuel algae growth.
  • pH – acidity/alkalinity of water.
  • Contaminants – including bacteria, metals and chemicals.
Ecology

Healthy water supports diverse plants and animals.

  • Fish – sensitive to oxygen levels and pollution.
  • Invertebrates – insects and larvae indicate long-term water health.
  • Habitats – reed beds, gravels and vegetation support wildlife.
  • Algae – natural but can bloom to harmful levels.
  • Biodiversity – the variety of species present.
Public health

What matters most for people using water for recreation.

  • Bathing water – designated sites with official classifications.
  • Health warnings – formal advice to avoid contact.
  • Swimming advice – practical guidance for today.
  • Water safety – currents, depth and cold water risks.

Water quality indicators

These are commonly measured. We translate them into plain English on location pages.

E. coli
Bacteria from gut waste. High levels suggest faecal contamination and a higher risk of illness if you swallow water.
Intestinal enterococci
Another bacteria used in bathing water standards. Indicates pollution from human or animal sources.
Dissolved oxygen
Oxygen available for aquatic life. Low levels can harm fish and indicate pollution.
pH
Measures acidity. Most rivers are slightly alkaline (pH 7–8). Extreme values can indicate problems.
Turbidity
Cloudiness caused by particles in water. High turbidity after rain is common but can carry pollutants.
Water temperature
Affects comfort, algae risk and oxygen levels. Warm stagnant water needs more caution.
Nitrate
A nutrient from fertilisers. Elevated levels can fuel algae and affect ecology.
Phosphate
Another nutrient. Often linked to sewage and agricultural runoff.
Ammonia
Toxic to aquatic life at high levels. Can indicate sewage pollution.
Conductivity
How well water conducts electricity. Indicates dissolved salts and minerals.
Chlorophyll
Indicates algae abundance. High levels may precede visible blooms.

Emerging indicators

Scientists are developing monitoring for newer concerns. A future Water Intelligence Service could include these where data becomes available.

  • PFAS – "forever chemicals" used in industry and consumer products, persistent in the environment.
  • Microplastics – tiny plastic particles from breakdown of larger plastics and synthetic fibres.
  • Pharmaceutical residues – medicines passing through sewage treatment into rivers.
  • Antibiotic resistant bacteria – bacteria that survive antibiotics, linked to wastewater.
  • Endocrine disrupting chemicals – chemicals that can interfere with hormones in wildlife and humans.
  • Biodiversity – broader ecological measures including species counts and habitat quality.

Ask a question if you want to know how any of these affect recreational use.
